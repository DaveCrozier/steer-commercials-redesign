RoRo vs. container shipping for used trucks: which should you choose?

6 min readExport logisticsUpdated 2026

If you're buying a used truck from the UK for export to Africa, the shipping method affects your landed cost, transit time and how the vehicle arrives. Here's how RoRo and container shipping actually compare.

The short answer

Most single tractor units and rigid trucks travel by RoRo (roll-on/roll-off) — it's cheaper and simpler for a vehicle that can be driven on and off the ship under its own power. Trailers, tippers with loose fittings, or shipments that include spare parts and tools are usually better suited to a container (FCL), where the load is sealed and locked for the full voyage.

How RoRo works

The truck is driven onto a specialised RoRo vessel at the UK port, secured on deck or below, and driven off again at the destination port. There's no loading crane, no container booking, and turnaround at both ends is fast — which is why it's usually the lower-cost option per vehicle.

How container (FCL) shipping works

The vehicle is loaded into a steel shipping container — typically a 40ft flat-rack or high-cube unit for a truck — sealed, and shipped as a single locked unit. It costs more, but the vehicle isn't exposed to the elements or handled loose at any port along the route.

A tractor unit going to Mombasa on its own usually goes RoRo. The same tractor paired with a tipping trailer, plus a box of spares, usually goes by container.

Which should you choose?

Work through it by what you're actually shipping:

  • Shipping one driveable tractor unit or rigid, nothing else? RoRo is usually the cheaper, simpler choice.
  • Shipping a trailer, tipper body, or anything that can't be driven on and off a ship? It needs a container.
  • Sending spares, tools, or extra parts alongside the vehicle? A container lets you consolidate them into one shipment.
  • Shipping to a port without RoRo facilities? Container is often the only option — worth checking before you commit to a vehicle.
  • Budget is the main constraint and the vehicle is driveable? RoRo will almost always land cheaper.

Documentation, either way

Regardless of shipping method, you'll need a bill of lading, export certificate, and — depending on the destination country — a pre-shipment or destination inspection certificate. We prepare UK-side export paperwork for every vehicle we sell; your clearing agent at the destination handles import clearance on their end.

Transit times and port capabilities vary by carrier and season — figures above are typical ranges, not guarantees. Confirm current sailing schedules and port RoRo capability with your shipping agent before booking.
